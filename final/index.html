Leaving College

For the last 20 months, the Covid-19 pandemic has had some impact on the lives of every person, one way or another. For students all over the United States, their education continued online until eventually falling back to in-person or hybrid classes. However, for many students, the pandemic was an obstacle that could not be overcome.
The number of high school and college dropouts rose along with the drop in college enrollment. Some students opted not to enter the college system at all.

When classes resumed in the fall, 2021 data shows that 25% of students decided to postpone their college experience during the pandemic. This is a worrying number as these are students who started their college careers and decided that it was going to be difficult to continue. Many of these students hail from low-income households.

A Washington post article dives into the life of one particular student who was the first to make it to college in her family. Online instruction forced Paige McConnell out of school as she had to means of attending. Her house had no Wi-Fi and public spaces such as libraries were closed. She was not the only one going through this. The chat details the number of students who had to postpone their education and why.

Based on this data, millions of students are unable to pay for their college education due to financial changes, such as job loss, as a result of the pandemic. Over 3.5 million students must take the roll of the caretaker after a relative either contracted the virus or their previous care arrangements changed for the worse. Of all the students surveyed, the overall numbers show that more students had to cancel their college plans than those who plan to continue their education.

One student, Natalia Cruze,(pictured with her nephew who she sometimes takes care of) shared that her college education took a direct hit from the pandemic.
“I was working a part-time job in the city while also trying to be a student up in Harlem. That itself is hard already, you know.”
The lifestyle, while difficult, was working out for Cruze up until the pandemic caused her to lose her job.
“Sometime in March, my boss told me I had to go, and I did not really have other places to work. Rent got expensive and school was a burden I couldn’t mentally handle or even pay for at the time, so I left.”

Not Entering College

Enrollment into college has decreased at all types of institutions at the undergraduate level since 2019 as well. The chart shows the overall decrease in enrollment from 2019 to 2021. Overall, the undergraduate enrollment has seen -7.8% decrease from fall 2019 to fall 2021.

Breaking down the data shows that all schools, public, private, 4-year, and 2-year colleges have seen a decrease. That being said, 2-year colleges, which are mainly community colleges where many low-income students attend, were hit the hardest. From 2019-2021 public 2-year colleges have seen a -14.8% decrease.

If you are or someone you know is having a difficult time with education, the National Dropout Prevention center Center offers resources to those in need.
